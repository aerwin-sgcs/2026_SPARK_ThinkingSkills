About this mapping

James Anderson's Learnership framework treats learning as a set of skills students actively develop, rather than a fixed capacity they either have or don't. Four interlocking ideas sit at its core: a position on the Mindset Continuum (from fixed to growth), the Habits of Mind (Costa & Kallick's 16 dispositions) that capable learners draw on, the integrated quality of the Agile Learner who deliberately develops their own capacity, and the Effective Effort they apply — strategy, feedback, and challenge at the right level.

The Stretch Tasks are designed for exactly that territory: problems whose answers aren't immediately known. The three narrative sections below show how the task set as a whole connects to the Mindset Continuum, the Agile Learner, and Effective Effort. The table that follows maps each task to its specific Habits of Mind — 1–3 primary Habits per task, kept tight to keep the mapping useful for planning.

The Mindset Continuum

Anderson refines Carol Dweck's binary fixed/growth model into a continuum: at one end, learners read struggle as evidence of a fixed limit ("I'm just not good at this"); at the other, they read struggle as the precise place where growth happens ("this is hard — that's the point"). Most students sit somewhere between, and their position shifts by subject, context, and how a task is framed.

Stretch Tasks are deliberately built with no clean answer. A student near the fixed end of the continuum will feel exposed and disengage; a student near the growth end will lean in. The tasks therefore work two ways at once: as a diagnostic (how a student responds to the discomfort reveals their current position) and as a gym (repeated practice with productive struggle is what shifts the position over time).

Where the tasks land

  • 4. Spot the Flaw in a Famous Model — requires accepting that authoritative knowledge can be wrong without becoming nihilistic about knowledge itself.
  • 14. Write the Other Side's Speech — fixed-mindset reaction is "I can't argue that, I disagree"; growth-mindset reaction is "what would change my mind?"
  • 23. When Should Engineers Refuse? — there is no safe answer; students must commit a position under uncertainty.
  • 26. Constrained Creation — cutting 100 words to 50 forces students past the first draft, exactly where fixed-mindset learners quit.
  • 28. The Museum of Mistakes — explicitly reframes failure as generative, the cornerstone of mindset shift.

The Agile Learner

Anderson uses Agile Learner for a student who actively, deliberately develops their own capacity to learn — combining the right mindset with the right habits, and applying both with strategic effort. Agile Learners seek out hard problems, treat feedback as information rather than judgement, and notice their own thinking as it happens.

The whole Stretch Tasks set is the kind of work an Agile Learner gravitates toward and a passive learner avoids. There is no recall to repeat, no formula to apply, no "right" answer to validate. Doing these tasks well — and especially doing them across sections, not just in comfort areas — is itself evidence of Agile Learner behaviour. Doing them poorly is informative too: it shows precisely where the Agile Learner muscles need building.

Where the tasks land

  • 1. Design a Study That Doesn't Exist Yet — students must self-direct the entire investigation; nothing is scaffolded.
  • 9. One Problem, Five Disciplines — agility is the explicit skill being tested: can you switch lenses fluently?
  • 15. Explain It at Five Levels — requires real-time adjustment of strategy as the audience changes.
  • 18. Define the Indefinable — three definitions, each defensible, tested against real cases: iterative, strategic thinking.
  • 22. Audit a System for Bias — demands the learner identify what data they would need before concluding, a hallmark Agile move.

Effective Effort

Anderson's framing of effort is precise: effective effort isn't just "trying hard." It's effort directed by strategy, calibrated to the right level of challenge (the zone of proximal development), informed by feedback, and applied to the right thing at the right time. Ineffective effort — re-reading, copying, polishing the easy parts — produces nothing.

Stretch Tasks reward effective effort and punish ineffective effort. There is nothing to memorise and nothing to polish; the work is the thinking. A student who throws an hour at a task without strategy produces a half-page of generic observation; a student who picks a lens, drafts, critiques their own draft, and revises produces something substantive. The gap between those two outputs is visible on the page.

Where the tasks land

  • 2. Reverse-Engineer a News Headline — without strategy ("find the source data first") students just opinionate; with strategy, they produce an audit.
  • 5. Reconstruct an Everyday Reaction — requires structured, layered thinking (substance → bonds → electrons) rather than a vague description.
  • 8. Build the Anti-Algorithm — students must specify decision rules and justify trade-offs; vague intentions don't survive.
  • 16. The Misleading Truth — sentence-by-sentence annotation forces effort at the right granularity, not the comfortable one.
  • 26. Constrained Creation — the cut from 100 to 50 words is pure deliberate practice on a single dimension (compression without loss).
